## 🤔 What is a Game Day?

Let's start with a simple analogy.

Remember fire drills at school? The alarm goes off, everyone walks out calmly, you practice the evacuation route, and then you go back inside. Nobody actually wants a fire — but you practice so that IF a fire happens, you know exactly what to do.

**A Game Day is the same thing, but for your software systems.**

Here's the idea:

```
Normal Day:
  Everything works → Team is happy → Nobody thinks about failures

Game Day:
  Team intentionally breaks things → Observes what happens →
  Discovers weaknesses → Fixes them → System becomes MORE resilient
```

### The Formal Definition:

A **Game Day** is a scheduled event where a team:
1. **Plans** specific failure scenarios
2. **Injects** those failures into their systems (staging or production)
3. **Observes** how the system and team respond
4. **Documents** findings, surprises, and gaps
5. **Creates action items** to fix the weaknesses found

### Why "Game Day"?

The term was popularized by **Amazon/AWS**. They run internal Game Days regularly to test their services. The word "Game" is intentional — it's meant to be **collaborative, fun, and low-blame**. Nobody gets in trouble for finding problems. In fact, finding problems is the GOAL.

## 🎯 Why Do Game Days & DR Drills Matter?

Here's a hard truth:

> **"Everyone has a plan until they get punched in the mouth."** — Mike Tyson

Your beautiful architecture diagrams, your runbooks, your monitoring dashboards — they all look great **until something actually breaks**. Then you discover:

- The runbook is outdated (it references a service that was renamed 6 months ago)
- Nobody knows who has access to the database admin credentials
- The backup that was "running daily" actually failed 3 weeks ago and nobody noticed
- The on-call engineer doesn't know how to restart the payment service
- The failover to the DR region takes 45 minutes instead of the expected 5 minutes

**Game Days find these gaps BEFORE a real incident does.**

### Real Statistics That Will Convince Your Boss:

| Stat | Source |
|------|--------|
| 93% of companies that lose their data center for 10+ days file for bankruptcy within one year | National Archives & Records Administration |
| The average cost of IT downtime is $5,600 per minute | Gartner |
| 75% of organizations have experienced a cloud outage in the past 3 years | Uptime Institute |
| Only 35% of organizations regularly test their DR plans | Forrester |
| Companies that regularly practice DR recover 4x faster than those that don't | Google SRE |

## 🏗️ Types of Game Days

Not all Game Days are the same. Here's a spectrum from easy to advanced:

### Level 1: Tabletop Exercise (No Real Failures)

```
Difficulty: ⭐ (Easiest)
Risk: None
Environment: Conference room / Zoom call
```

This is a **discussion-based exercise**. No actual systems are touched. The team sits around a table (or Zoom) and walks through a hypothetical failure scenario.

**Example:**
> "Imagine it's 2 AM on a Saturday. You get paged. The database cluster in us-east-1 is completely down. All 3 replicas are unreachable. Walk me through what you would do."

**What you discover:**
- Does everyone know who to contact?
- Do people know where the runbooks are?
- Can people describe the recovery steps from memory?
- Are there disagreements about the correct procedure?

**This is the BEST way to start if your team has never done a Game Day before.** Zero risk, huge learning.

### Level 2: Staging Environment Game Day

```
Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐ (Medium)
Risk: Low (staging only)
Environment: Staging/pre-production cluster
```

Now you actually inject real failures — but only in staging. This is where you use tools like Chaos Mesh, Litmus, or Gremlin.

**Example:**
> Kill the payment service, add 500ms latency to the database, and see if the checkout flow still works.

### Level 3: Production Game Day (Controlled)

```
Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Hard)
Risk: Medium (production, but controlled)
Environment: Production (with guardrails)
```

The ultimate test — inject failures in production. But with strict controls:
- During business hours (when the team is awake and ready)
- With a clear rollback plan
- With monitoring dashboards open
- With the ability to halt immediately
- Starting with the smallest possible blast radius

**Example:**
> Kill one pod of the payment service in production during low-traffic hours. Monitor error rates, latency, and user impact.

### Level 4: Full-Scale Disaster Recovery Drill

```
Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Expert)
Risk: Medium-High (DR failover involves real infrastructure)
Environment: Production + DR site
```

This is the big one. You simulate a complete regional failure and test your ability to fail over to the DR site.

**Example:**
> Simulate a complete failure of the primary AWS region (us-east-1). Fail over ALL services to the DR region (us-west-2). Validate data integrity, then fail back.

## 📋 How to Plan a Game Day — Step by Step

### Step 1: Define the Scope

Before anything else, answer these questions:

| Question | Example Answer |
|----------|---------------|
| **What are we testing?** | "We're testing if our system can survive the loss of the primary database" |
| **Which systems are involved?** | "Payment service, Order service, PostgreSQL primary, PostgreSQL replicas" |
| **What environment?** | "Staging" (or "Production — low-traffic window") |
| **What's the hypothesis?** | "If the primary database goes down, the app should automatically fail over to the read replica within 30 seconds, with zero data loss" |
| **What's the blast radius?** | "Only the payment service in the default namespace" |
| **What's the rollback plan?** | "Restore primary database from snapshot, kubectl rollout undo" |
| **Who needs to be involved?** | "Backend team, DBA, SRE on-call, product owner informed" |
| **When?** | "Tuesday 2 PM — 4 PM (low-traffic period)" |

### Step 2: Write the Game Day Plan

Here's a real-world Game Day plan template:

```markdown
# Game Day Plan: Database Failover Test
## Date: 2024-03-15, 2:00 PM - 4:00 PM EST

## Objective
Test our PostgreSQL automatic failover from primary to replica 
when the primary becomes unavailable.

## Hypothesis
When the primary PostgreSQL instance is terminated:
1. The connection pooler (PgBouncer) detects the failure within 10 seconds
2. The failover to the replica completes within 30 seconds
3. The application experiences < 1% error rate during failover
4. No data is lost (RPO = 0)
5. Total recovery time < 1 minute (RTO < 60s)

## Team
| Role | Person | Responsibility |
|------|--------|---------------|
| **Game Day Lead** | Alice (SRE) | Coordinates the exercise, makes go/no-go decisions |
| **Attacker** | Bob (SRE) | Injects the failure |
| **Observer — App** | Carol (Backend) | Monitors application metrics and logs |
| **Observer — DB** | Dave (DBA) | Monitors database metrics, replication lag |
| **Observer — Infra** | Eve (Platform) | Monitors infrastructure (CPU, memory, network) |
| **Scribe** | Frank (SRE) | Documents everything in real-time |
| **Communications** | Grace (PM) | Informs stakeholders if needed |

## Pre-Game Checklist
- [ ] All team members confirmed availability
- [ ] Monitoring dashboards open (Grafana, Datadog, PagerDuty)
- [ ] Runbooks reviewed and accessible
- [ ] Backup verified (last successful backup < 1 hour old)
- [ ] Rollback plan documented and understood
- [ ] Stakeholders informed (engineering manager, product)
- [ ] Staging environment verified as working
- [ ] Chaos tool (Gremlin/Chaos Mesh) ready and tested
- [ ] Communication channel set up (Slack: #game-day-march)

## Experiment Steps
1. [T+0 min] Game Day Lead confirms GO/NO-GO
2. [T+2 min] Observe baseline metrics (error rate, latency, throughput)
3. [T+5 min] Attacker injects failure: terminate primary PostgreSQL pod
4. [T+5 min] All observers start monitoring their areas
5. [T+5-10 min] Observe: Does PgBouncer detect the failure?
6. [T+5-10 min] Observe: Does automatic failover trigger?
7. [T+10 min] Observe: Is the app serving traffic from the new primary?
8. [T+15 min] Observe: What's the error rate? Any data loss?
9. [T+20 min] Validate: Run data integrity checks
10. [T+30 min] Restore: Bring up a new replica from the new primary
11. [T+45 min] Debrief: Discuss findings

## Abort Criteria (STOP the game day if):
- Error rate exceeds 10% for more than 2 minutes
- Any production customer reports issues
- Data loss is detected
- The team feels unsafe continuing

## Success Criteria
- [ ] Failover completes in < 60 seconds
- [ ] Error rate stays below 1%
- [ ] No data loss detected
- [ ] App recovers without manual intervention
```

### Step 3: Run the Game Day

Here's what it looks like in practice:

```
2:00 PM — Game Day Lead: "Alright everyone, welcome to Game Day! 
         Let's do a quick roll call and dashboard check."

2:02 PM — All: "Dashboards open, all green, baseline metrics captured."

2:05 PM — Game Day Lead: "GO. Bob, inject the failure."

2:05 PM — Attacker: "Injecting now. Terminating primary PostgreSQL pod."
         $ kubectl delete pod postgres-primary-0 --force --grace-period=0

2:05 PM — Observer (DB): "Primary is down. Watching for failover..."

2:06 PM — Observer (DB): "Patroni detected the failure! Promoting replica..."

2:06 PM — Observer (App): "Seeing connection errors. Error rate spiking to 8%."

2:07 PM — Observer (DB): "Failover complete! New primary is accepting writes."

2:07 PM — Observer (App): "Error rate dropping... 3%... 1%... back to normal!"

2:08 PM — Observer (Infra): "PgBouncer reconnected to new primary. All healthy."

2:08 PM — Scribe: "Failover took 47 seconds. Max error rate was 8%. 
          No data loss detected. All connections restored by T+3 minutes."

2:10 PM — Game Day Lead: "Excellent! Let's move to data integrity validation."

2:15 PM — Observer (DB): "Running checksums... All data consistent. Zero data loss."

2:20 PM — Game Day Lead: "Great work everyone. Let's debrief."
```

### Step 4: Debrief (The Most Important Part!)

The debrief is where the **real learning happens**. Hold it immediately after the Game Day while everything is fresh.

#### Debrief Template:

```markdown
# Game Day Debrief: Database Failover Test
## Date: 2024-03-15

## Summary
| Metric | Expected | Actual | Pass/Fail |
|--------|----------|--------|-----------|
| Failover time | < 60s | 47s | ✅ PASS |
| Max error rate | < 1% | 8% | ❌ FAIL |
| Data loss | 0 | 0 | ✅ PASS |
| RTO | < 60s | 47s | ✅ PASS |
| Auto-recovery | Yes | Yes | ✅ PASS |

## What Went Well
- Failover completed within our RTO target
- Zero data loss — replication was up to date
- Team communication was excellent
- Patroni detected and handled the failure automatically

## What Surprised Us
- Error rate spiked to 8% (expected < 1%)
  → Root cause: PgBouncer connection pool took 15 seconds to detect 
    the dead connection and retry with the new primary
  → Fix: Reduce PgBouncer `server_connect_timeout` from 15s to 3s
  
- Two application instances didn't reconnect automatically
  → Root cause: Connection pool library caches the DNS for 5 minutes
  → Fix: Set DNS TTL to 10 seconds in the app config

## Action Items
| # | Action | Owner | Priority | Due Date |
|---|--------|-------|----------|----------|
| 1 | Reduce PgBouncer connect timeout to 3s | Dave (DBA) | P1 | March 18 |
| 2 | Fix DNS caching in app connection pool | Carol (Backend) | P1 | March 18 |
| 3 | Update runbook with accurate failover time | Frank (SRE) | P2 | March 20 |
| 4 | Add PgBouncer reconnection latency to dashboard | Eve (Platform) | P2 | March 22 |
| 5 | Schedule follow-up Game Day to validate fixes | Alice (SRE) | P2 | March 29 |

## Lessons Learned
1. Connection pool behavior during failover is often overlooked — test it!
2. DNS caching can cause "sticky connections" to dead endpoints
3. Our monitoring caught the issue quickly — good dashboards matter
4. Practice makes perfect — this Game Day was much smoother than our first one
```

## 🔥 Disaster Recovery (DR) Drills — The Big One

DR Drills are a specific type of Game Day focused on **major disaster scenarios** — think "entire region goes down" or "database is corrupted and we need to restore from backup."

### Common DR Scenarios to Test:

| Scenario | What You're Testing |
|----------|-------------------|
| **Region Failover** | Can we fail over all services to the DR region? |
| **Database Restore from Backup** | Can we restore the database from S3/GCS backup? How long does it take? |
| **Complete Cluster Rebuild** | Can we rebuild the entire Kubernetes cluster from IaC (Terraform)? |
| **DNS Failover** | If Route 53 / Cloud DNS detects a failure, does DNS fail over correctly? |
| **Data Center Power Failure** | If an entire availability zone goes down, do services failover? |
| **Ransomware Recovery** | If all data is encrypted by ransomware, can we restore from immutable backups? |
| **Key Person Unavailability** | What if the ONE person who knows the system is on vacation? Can others recover? |

---

### DR Drill Example 1: Database Backup & Restore

This is the most critical DR drill. If you can't restore your data, nothing else matters.

#### The Plan:

```markdown
# DR Drill: PostgreSQL Backup Restore Test

## Objective
Validate that we can restore our production PostgreSQL database 
from the latest automated backup in S3.

## Metrics to Measure
- RPO (Recovery Point Objective): How much data do we lose?
  Target: < 15 minutes
- RTO (Recovery Time Objective): How long does recovery take?
  Target: < 1 hour
```

#### Step-by-Step Execution:

```bash
# Step 1: Verify the latest backup exists
aws s3 ls s3://my-company-db-backups/postgres/ --recursive | tail -5
# Expected output:
# 2024-03-15 01:00:00   5.2 GB  postgres/daily/backup-2024-03-15-0100.sql.gz
# 2024-03-15 02:00:00   125 MB  postgres/wal/wal-2024-03-15-0200.tar.gz

# Step 2: Note the backup timestamp
# Latest backup: 2024-03-15 01:00:00 (1 AM)
# Current time:  2024-03-15 14:30:00 (2:30 PM)
# Potential data loss: 13.5 hours (WAL archives reduce this)

# Step 3: Create a restore environment
# Spin up a new PostgreSQL instance for testing
kubectl apply -f - <<EOF
apiVersion: v1
kind: Pod
metadata:
  name: postgres-restore-test
  namespace: dr-testing
spec:
  containers:
  - name: postgres
    image: postgres:15
    env:
    - name: POSTGRES_PASSWORD
      value: "restore-test-password"
    volumeMounts:
    - name: data
      mountPath: /var/lib/postgresql/data
  volumes:
  - name: data
    emptyDir:
      sizeLimit: 50Gi
EOF

# Step 4: Download the backup
kubectl exec -n dr-testing postgres-restore-test -- bash -c "
  apt-get update && apt-get install -y awscli
  aws s3 cp s3://my-company-db-backups/postgres/daily/backup-2024-03-15-0100.sql.gz /tmp/
"

# Step 5: Restore the backup
echo "Starting restore at $(date)"
kubectl exec -n dr-testing postgres-restore-test -- bash -c "
  gunzip -c /tmp/backup-2024-03-15-0100.sql.gz | psql -U postgres
"
echo "Restore completed at $(date)"

# Step 6: Apply WAL archives (Point-in-Time Recovery)
kubectl exec -n dr-testing postgres-restore-test -- bash -c "
  aws s3 cp s3://my-company-db-backups/postgres/wal/ /tmp/wal/ --recursive
  # Apply WAL files to bring the database to the latest point in time
"

# Step 7: Validate the data
kubectl exec -n dr-testing postgres-restore-test -- psql -U postgres -c "
  SELECT COUNT(*) FROM orders WHERE created_at > '2024-03-15';
  SELECT MAX(created_at) FROM orders;
  -- Check: does the latest order match what we expect?
"

# Step 8: Measure results
# - Download time: 8 minutes (5.2 GB from S3)
# - Restore time: 22 minutes
# - WAL replay time: 5 minutes
# - Total RTO: 35 minutes ✅ (under 1 hour target)
# - RPO: 3 minutes ✅ (WAL archives brought us to 3 minutes before current time)

# Step 9: Clean up
kubectl delete pod postgres-restore-test -n dr-testing
```

#### Document the Results:

```markdown
## DR Drill Results: Database Backup Restore

| Metric | Target | Actual | Status |
|--------|--------|--------|--------|
| RPO | < 15 min | 3 min | ✅ PASS |
| RTO | < 1 hour | 35 min | ✅ PASS |
| Data Integrity | 100% | 100% | ✅ PASS |
| Backup Size | N/A | 5.2 GB | ℹ️ INFO |

## Issues Found:
1. The awscli wasn't pre-installed in the restore container 
   → Added to the DR container image
2. WAL archive folder had files from 6 months ago (never cleaned up) 
   → Added lifecycle policy to S3 bucket
3. Restore user didn't have the right IAM permissions initially 
   → Created a dedicated DR IAM role with required permissions
```

This is the ultimate DR test. You simulate an entire AWS/Azure/GCP region going down and fail over to your DR region.

#### The Drill Plan:

```markdown
# DR Drill: Full Region Failover

## Phase 1: Preparation (1 hour before)
- [ ] Verify DR region infrastructure is up (scaled down but running)
- [ ] Verify database replication lag < 1 second
- [ ] Verify Route 53 health checks are active
- [ ] Inform all stakeholders
- [ ] Open all monitoring dashboards
- [ ] Prepare rollback plan

## Phase 2: Simulate Regional Failure
- [ ] Scale down all deployments in us-east-1 to 0 replicas
- [ ] OR: Use Gremlin to blackhole all traffic in us-east-1

## Phase 3: Observe Failover
- [ ] Route 53 health check detects failure (target: < 30 seconds)
- [ ] DNS failover to us-west-2 triggers (target: < 60 seconds)
- [ ] DR region scales up (target: < 5 minutes)
- [ ] Database promotes replica to primary (target: < 2 minutes)
- [ ] Application is serving traffic from DR region (target: < 10 minutes)

## Phase 4: Validate
- [ ] All APIs return correct responses
- [ ] Data integrity check — no data lost
- [ ] All integrations working (payment provider, email, etc.)
- [ ] Performance is acceptable from DR region

## Phase 5: Failback
- [ ] Restore us-east-1 infrastructure
- [ ] Re-establish database replication (us-west-2 → us-east-1)
- [ ] Wait for replication to catch up
- [ ] Fail DNS back to us-east-1
- [ ] Scale down us-west-2 to standby mode

## Phase 6: Debrief
- [ ] Document total RTO
- [ ] Document any data loss (RPO)
- [ ] List all issues found
- [ ] Create action items
```

#### Execution Commands:

```bash
# Phase 2: Simulate the failure
# Option A: Scale everything down in us-east-1
kubectl config use-context us-east-1-cluster
kubectl scale deployment --all --replicas=0 -n production

# Option B: Use Gremlin to blackhole network (more realistic)
gremlin attack blackhole --length 600 --tag region=us-east-1

# Phase 3: Monitor the failover
# Watch Route 53 health checks
aws route53 get-health-check-status --health-check-id HC_ID_HERE

# Watch DR region scaling
kubectl config use-context us-west-2-cluster
kubectl get pods -n production -w  # Watch pods coming up

# Check database promotion
kubectl logs -n database -l app=patroni --tail=100

# Phase 4: Validate
# Test the API from DR region
curl -v https://api.mycompany.com/health
curl -v https://api.mycompany.com/api/v1/orders?limit=1

# Run data integrity checks
kubectl exec -n database postgres-0 -- psql -U app -c "
  SELECT COUNT(*) as total_orders FROM orders;
  SELECT MAX(id) as latest_order FROM orders;
"

# Phase 5: Failback
# Restore us-east-1
kubectl config use-context us-east-1-cluster
kubectl scale deployment frontend --replicas=3 -n production
kubectl scale deployment api --replicas=5 -n production

# Wait for pods to be ready
kubectl rollout status deployment/frontend -n production
kubectl rollout status deployment/api -n production

# Re-establish replication
# (This depends on your database setup — Patroni, RDS, etc.)

# Update Route 53 back to us-east-1
aws route53 change-resource-record-sets --hosted-zone-id Z_ID \
  --change-batch '{
    "Changes": [{
      "Action": "UPSERT",
      "ResourceRecordSet": {
        "Name": "api.mycompany.com",
        "Type": "A",
        "AliasTarget": {
          "HostedZoneId": "Z_US_EAST_1",
          "DNSName": "alb-us-east-1.amazonaws.com",
          "EvaluateTargetHealth": true
        }
      }
    }]
  }'
```

## 🏃 Running Effective Game Days — Best Practices

### Before the Game Day:

| Practice | Why |
|----------|-----|
| **Start with tabletop exercises** | If your team has never done this, start with a discussion — no risk |
| **Define clear hypotheses** | "We believe X will happen when we inject Y" — makes the experiment scientific |
| **Pick the RIGHT time** | Low-traffic periods, when the full team is available, NOT on Fridays |
| **Inform stakeholders** | Let your manager, product team, and support team know |
| **Have a rollback plan** | Know EXACTLY how to undo the chaos if things go wrong |
| **Check your monitoring** | Make sure dashboards and alerts are working BEFORE you start |
| **Verify backups** | Confirm your latest backup is recent and restorable |
| **Assign clear roles** | Everyone should know their job (attacker, observer, scribe, lead) |

### During the Game Day:

| Practice | Why |
|----------|-----|
| **One person leads** | The Game Day Lead makes all decisions — avoids confusion |
| **Communicate constantly** | Use a dedicated Slack channel, speak clearly about what you observe |
| **Document EVERYTHING** | The scribe writes down timestamps, observations, and surprises |
| **Start small** | Kill one pod before killing ten. Add 100ms latency before 5 seconds |
| **Have the halt button ready** | Know EXACTLY how to stop the chaos immediately |
| **Don't fix during the game** | Observe and document first. Fix later. The goal is to find problems, not solve them in real-time |
| **Watch the clock** | Time-box the exercise. Don't let it drag on |

### After the Game Day:

| Practice | Why |
|----------|-----|
| **Debrief immediately** | While everything is fresh in everyone's mind |
| **No blame** | This is blameless. Every finding is a gift, not a failure |
| **Create action items** | Every finding should become a ticket with an owner and due date |
| **Share the results** | Write a summary and share it with the broader engineering team |
| **Schedule the next one** | Make Game Days a regular practice, not a one-time event |
| **Track improvement** | Compare results across Game Days to show progress |

## 📅 Game Day Cadence — How Often?

| Type | Recommended Frequency |
|------|----------------------|
| **Tabletop exercises** | Monthly |
| **Staging Game Days** | Bi-weekly to Monthly |
| **Production Game Days (small)** | Monthly |
| **Production Game Days (large)** | Quarterly |
| **Full DR Drills** | Quarterly to Semi-annually |
| **Backup Restore Tests** | Monthly |

### Netflix's Approach:

Netflix runs chaos **continuously**:
- **Chaos Monkey** kills random instances every day
- **Chaos Kong** simulates entire region failures monthly

You don't have to be Netflix on day one. Start with monthly tabletop exercises and work your way up.

## 🏢 Real-World Game Day Stories

### Story 1: The Backup That Wasn't

```
Company: A mid-size SaaS company

Game Day Plan: Test database restore from S3 backup

What They Expected: 
  "We have automated daily backups. Restore should take 30 minutes."

What Actually Happened:
  - Downloaded the backup file → It was 0 bytes 😱
  - The backup job had been failing silently for 3 WEEKS
  - The monitoring alert for backup failures was going to an email 
    distribution list that nobody checked
  - They had NO working backup of their production database

How They Found Out: During the Game Day drill

What They Fixed:
  1. Added a Slack alert for backup failures (not just email)
  2. Added a daily check that verifies backup file size > minimum
  3. Added monthly automated restore tests
  4. Added backup age to the ops dashboard

Impact: Avoided potential total data loss disaster
```

### Story 2: The 45-Minute Failover

```
Company: A financial services company

Game Day Plan: Test region failover (us-east-1 → us-west-2)

What They Expected:
  "Our DNS failover should complete in 5 minutes"

What Actually Happened:
  - Route 53 detected failure in 30 seconds ✅
  - DNS failover happened in 60 seconds ✅
  - DR region started scaling up... but pods were stuck in Pending ❌
  - Why? The DR region didn't have enough nodes
  - Cluster Autoscaler started provisioning nodes... 
    but it took 8 minutes
  - THEN the pods needed to pull container images...
    but the images weren't cached in the DR region
  - Image pulls took 12 minutes (large images, cross-region pull)
  - THEN the database replica needed to be promoted...
    but the promotion script had a bug and failed
  - Manual database promotion took 15 minutes
  - Total failover time: 45 minutes (expected: 5 minutes!)

What They Fixed:
  1. Pre-pulled container images in DR region (using DaemonSet)
  2. Pre-warmed DR nodes (kept minimum 2 nodes always running)
  3. Fixed the database promotion script and tested it
  4. Reduced container image sizes (from 2GB to 400MB)
  5. Re-ran the DR drill → Failover time dropped to 4 minutes ✅

Impact: Reduced RTO from 45 minutes to 4 minutes
```

### Story 3: The Key Person Problem

```
Company: A startup with 30 engineers

Game Day Plan: Tabletop exercise — "What if our lead SRE is unreachable?"

What They Expected:
  "Other engineers can handle incidents"

What Actually Happened (in the tabletop discussion):
  - "Who knows how to access the production database?"
    → Only the lead SRE
  - "Who has the credentials for the cloud admin account?"
    → Only the lead SRE  
  - "Who knows how to restart the Kafka cluster?"
    → Only the lead SRE
  - "Where are the runbooks?"
    → "I think they're on the lead SRE's laptop..."

What They Fixed:
  1. Documented ALL operational procedures in a shared wiki
  2. Created shared credential management (HashiCorp Vault)
  3. Cross-trained 3 other engineers on critical procedures
  4. Implemented pair on-call (two people always on-call together)
  5. Required that any production procedure must be documented 
     in the runbook

Impact: Eliminated single point of failure (the "bus factor" problem)
```

## 📊 Measuring Game Day Success Over Time

Track these metrics across Game Days to show improvement:

```
Game Day #1 (January):
  - Database failover time: 12 minutes
  - Issues found: 7
  - Critical issues: 3

Game Day #2 (March):
  - Database failover time: 5 minutes (improved!)
  - Issues found: 4 (fewer issues = system is getting better)
  - Critical issues: 1

Game Day #3 (May):
  - Database failover time: 2 minutes (great!)
  - Issues found: 2
  - Critical issues: 0

Trend: Each Game Day finds fewer issues → System is becoming more resilient
```

Create a simple dashboard or spreadsheet to track this. It's a powerful way to show leadership that Game Days provide real, measurable value.

## 🚫 Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Why It's Bad | What to Do Instead |
|---------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Never testing in production** | Staging doesn't match production exactly | Gradually work up to small production tests |
| **No rollback plan** | If things go wrong, you're stuck | ALWAYS have a documented rollback plan |
| **Blaming people for findings** | People stop participating | Make it blameless — findings are gifts |
| **Not following up on action items** | Same issues appear in the next Game Day | Track action items in JIRA/Linear with due dates |
| **Making it too complex** | Overwhelming the team | Start simple, increase complexity over time |
| **Doing it once and never again** | One-time knowledge fades | Make it a regular practice (monthly/quarterly) |
| **No monitoring during the drill** | You can't see what's happening | Set up dashboards BEFORE the Game Day |
| **Not involving the whole team** | Only SRE learns, devs don't | Include developers, product managers, and support |
| **Skipping the debrief** | Findings are lost | ALWAYS do a debrief immediately after |
| **Testing only known failure modes** | You miss creative failures | Also test unexpected scenarios |

## 🔑 Key Takeaways

1. **Game Days** are planned events where you intentionally inject failures to find weaknesses — like fire drills for your systems.
2. **Start with tabletop exercises** (zero risk) before progressing to staging and then production Game Days.
3. **DR Drills** specifically test disaster recovery — backup restores, region failovers, and data integrity.
4. **Every Game Day needs a plan** — hypothesis, scope, roles, rollback plan, and abort criteria.
5. **The debrief is the most important part** — document findings, create action items, and track improvements.
6. **Make it blameless** — the goal is to find problems, not blame people.
7. **Regularly test backups** — "Untested backups are not backups." Test restore monthly.
8. **Track progress over time** — each Game Day should find fewer issues, proving your system is getting more resilient.
9. **Involve the whole team** — developers, SREs, DBAs, product managers — everyone benefits.
10. **Make it a habit** — one-time Game Days don't build lasting resilience. Regular practice does.
