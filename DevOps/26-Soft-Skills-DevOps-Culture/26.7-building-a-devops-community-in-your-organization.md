## 1. Why Build a DevOps Community?

Let's be honest — you can buy the best tools in the world, set up the most sophisticated CI/CD pipelines, and have Kubernetes running in production. But if **people don't talk to each other**, don't share knowledge, and don't collaborate across team boundaries, **DevOps will fail**.

DevOps is not a tool. It's not even a process. At its core, DevOps is a **culture**. And culture doesn't happen automatically — it needs to be **intentionally built, nurtured, and sustained**.

### Why does it matter?

| Problem Without a Community | Benefit With a Community |
|---|---|
| Teams work in silos — "not my problem" mentality | Cross-team collaboration becomes natural |
| Same mistakes are repeated across teams | Lessons are shared and institutionalized |
| New hires take months to get productive | Onboarding is faster with shared knowledge |
| Knowledge is locked in individual heads | Knowledge is distributed and documented |
| Innovation is slow — nobody shares experiments | People share ideas, tools, and discoveries freely |
| Burnout — people feel alone solving problems | Support network — "I had the same issue, here's what I did" |
| Resistance to change — "we've always done it this way" | Change is driven bottom-up by excited practitioners |

### The simple truth:

> **A DevOps community turns individual expertise into collective intelligence.**

Think about it this way:
- If ONE person knows how to debug a tricky Kubernetes networking issue, that's helpful when they're available.
- If they **share** that knowledge with 50 people through a community, that's **50x the capability**.

## 3. The Foundation — Getting Started

Building a community from scratch can feel overwhelming. But here's the good news: **you don't need permission to start**. You just need a few passionate people and a willingness to share.

### Step-by-Step: Starting Your DevOps Community

#### Step 1: Start Small — Find Your First 3-5 People

You don't need the whole company. Find 3-5 like-minded people who are already passionate about DevOps. They might be:
- The person who set up the Jenkins pipeline
- The one who's always helping with Docker issues on Slack
- The developer who keeps asking "why can't we automate this?"
- The ops person who loves Terraform

> **Tip:** Look for energy, not titles. A junior engineer who's excited about DevOps is more valuable to a community than a senior architect who's too busy to participate.

#### Step 2: Define Your Purpose

Every community needs a clear "why." Write a simple mission statement:

**Example:**
> *"Our DevOps community exists to share knowledge, improve our engineering practices, and create a culture of collaboration across all teams."*

Keep it simple. Keep it human.

#### Step 3: Pick a Communication Channel

You need ONE primary place where conversations happen:

| Tool | Best For |
|------|----------|
| **Slack** | Quick discussions, async Q&A, sharing links |
| **Microsoft Teams** | Organizations already on Microsoft ecosystem |
| **Discord** | More informal, threaded conversations |
| **Mattermost** | Self-hosted, open-source Slack alternative |

Create channels like:
- `#devops-community` — General discussion
- `#devops-help` — Ask questions, get help
- `#devops-wins` — Celebrate successes and wins
- `#devops-learning` — Share articles, courses, videos

#### Step 4: Schedule Your First Event

Don't overthink it. Your first event can be as simple as:

> *"Hey, let's grab lunch on Friday and talk about how we can improve our deployment process."*

That's it. That's a community event.

#### Step 5: Get Leadership Buy-In (But Don't Wait for It)

Leadership support helps, but don't wait for it. **Start the community, show value, THEN get support.**

When you do approach leadership, frame it as:
- "This will reduce incident resolution time"
- "This will speed up onboarding for new engineers"
- "This will prevent repeated mistakes across teams"
- "This costs nothing — just an hour of people's time every two weeks"

## 4. Identifying Champions & Advocates

Champions are the **heartbeat** of your community. They're the people who show up, share, answer questions, and pull others in.

### Who Makes a Good Champion?

| Trait | Why It Matters |
|-------|----------------|
| **Passionate about DevOps** | They'll keep the energy going even when things are slow |
| **Good communicators** | They can explain complex things simply |
| **Respected by peers** | People listen to them and trust their recommendations |
| **Willing to invest time** | Community building takes real effort |
| **Diverse backgrounds** | You want champions from Dev, Ops, QA, Security — not just one team |

### How to Find Them

1. **Look at your Slack/Teams channels** — Who's always answering questions?
2. **Check your internal wiki** — Who's writing documentation voluntarily?
3. **Talk to managers** — "Who on your team is really into DevOps?"
4. **Post an open call** — "We're starting a DevOps community. Interested? Join us!"
5. **Watch for the "after-hours learners"** — People who tinker with new tools on their own time

### The Champion Network

```
          🏆 Community Lead
              │
    ┌─────────┼─────────┐
    │         │         │
🌟 Champion  🌟 Champion  🌟 Champion
 (Dev Team)  (Ops Team)  (Security)
    │         │         │
👥 Members  👥 Members  👥 Members
```

Each champion becomes the **bridge** between the community and their team. They:
- Bring topics and pain points from their team
- Take learnings back to their team
- Recruit new members
- Present at community events

### Recognizing Champions

People do this for free. Recognize them:
- **Public shout-outs** in all-hands meetings
- **Internal blog posts** featuring their contributions
- **Conference tickets** as a thank-you
- **"DevOps Champion" badges** on internal profiles
- **Nomination for awards** or promotions
- **Simply saying "thank you"** — it goes a long way

## 5. Creating a Community of Practice (CoP)

A **Community of Practice (CoP)** is a formal structure for your DevOps community. It's a concept from knowledge management that's been adopted by many tech companies.

### What is a CoP?

A CoP is a **group of people who share a concern or passion for something they do, and learn how to do it better through regular interaction.**

Three key elements:
1. **Domain** — The shared area of interest (DevOps, Cloud, Kubernetes, etc.)
2. **Community** — The group of people who care about this domain
3. **Practice** — The shared tools, techniques, methods, and knowledge

### CoP Structure

```yaml
# Community of Practice — Charter Template

name: "DevOps Community of Practice"
domain: "DevOps engineering practices, tooling, and culture"
mission: >
  To share knowledge, improve engineering practices,
  and foster a culture of collaboration and continuous
  improvement across all engineering teams.

governance:
  lead: "Jane Smith (Principal DevOps Engineer)"
  co-leads:
    - "Bob Chen (Senior SRE)"
    - "Maria Rodriguez (Platform Engineer)"
  sponsor: "VP of Engineering — Tom Wilson"

membership:
  open_to: "Anyone in engineering, QA, or product"
  current_members: 45
  how_to_join: "Post in #devops-community or talk to any lead"

cadence:
  meetups: "Every 2 weeks, Thursday 3-4 PM"
  workshops: "Monthly, last Friday of the month"
  retrospective: "Quarterly"

channels:
  primary: "#devops-community (Slack)"
  help: "#devops-help (Slack)"
  wiki: "Confluence > Engineering > DevOps CoP"

goals_this_quarter:
  - "Standardize CI/CD pipeline templates across teams"
  - "Create onboarding runbook for new engineers"
  - "Host 2 internal workshops on Kubernetes troubleshooting"
  - "Reduce mean time to recovery (MTTR) by 20%"
```

### How a CoP Meeting Typically Runs

```
📅 DevOps CoP — Bi-Weekly Meeting (1 hour)
│
├── 🕐 0:00 - 0:05  Welcome & Updates
│   └── What's new? Any announcements?
│
├── 🕐 0:05 - 0:25  Main Talk / Demo (20 min)
│   └── One person presents a topic
│   └── Examples:
│       ├── "How we migrated to GitHub Actions"
│       ├── "Debugging OOM kills in Kubernetes"
│       └── "Terraform module best practices"
│
├── 🕐 0:25 - 0:35  Q&A / Discussion (10 min)
│   └── Open floor for questions
│
├── 🕐 0:35 - 0:50  Lightning Round (15 min)
│   └── Quick shares from anyone:
│       ├── "I found this cool tool..."
│       ├── "We hit this issue and here's how we solved it..."
│       └── "Has anyone tried X?"
│
└── 🕐 0:50 - 1:00  Action Items & Next Meeting Planning
    └── Who's presenting next time?
    └── Any follow-up items?
```

## 6. Knowledge Sharing Mechanisms

A community is only as strong as the knowledge it shares. Here are proven mechanisms:

### 6.1 Internal Tech Talks

- **Format:** 20-30 minute presentation + 10 min Q&A
- **Frequency:** Bi-weekly or monthly
- **Topics:** Anything DevOps-related — tools, processes, war stories, lessons learned
- **Key rule:** ALWAYS record them for people who can't attend

**Example schedule:**
| Week | Speaker | Topic |
|------|---------|-------|
| Week 1 | Alice (Backend) | "How we cut deployment time from 45 min to 5 min" |
| Week 3 | Bob (SRE) | "Prometheus alerting — what works and what doesn't" |
| Week 5 | Charlie (Security) | "Container image scanning with Trivy — demo" |
| Week 7 | Dana (Platform) | "ArgoCD App of Apps — our GitOps journey" |

### 6.2 Lunch-and-Learns (Brown Bag Sessions)

- **Format:** Informal, over lunch, 30-45 minutes
- **Vibe:** Casual, no slides required (whiteboard is fine)
- **Great for:** Sharing war stories, discussing new tools, brainstorming
- **Food helps!** If budget allows, provide lunch. People show up for food.

### 6.3 Internal Blog / Tech Articles

Encourage people to write internal blog posts about:
- How they solved a complex problem
- A new tool they evaluated
- Best practices they discovered
- Post-incident learnings

**Platform options:**
- **Confluence** — Most common in enterprise
- **Notion** — Modern, flexible, great for team wikis
- **GitBook** — Developer-friendly documentation
- **Internal WordPress/Ghost** — If you want a proper blog feel
- **Just a Git repo with Markdown files** — Simple and effective!

### 6.4 Knowledge Base & Wiki

Build a centralized knowledge base:

```
📂 DevOps Knowledge Base
│
├── 📁 Runbooks
│   ├── How to deploy to production
│   ├── How to rollback a deployment
│   ├── How to handle a database failover
│   └── On-call escalation procedures
│
├── 📁 How-To Guides
│   ├── Setting up a new microservice
│   ├── Adding a new service to monitoring
│   ├── Creating a Terraform module
│   └── Configuring RBAC in Kubernetes
│
├── 📁 Architecture Decision Records (ADRs)
│   ├── ADR-001: Why we chose ArgoCD over Flux
│   ├── ADR-002: Database migration strategy
│   └── ADR-003: Multi-region deployment approach
│
├── 📁 Post-Incident Reviews
│   ├── 2026-01-15: Production outage — root cause analysis
│   └── 2026-03-22: DNS misconfiguration incident
│
└── 📁 Tool Guides
    ├── Docker cheat sheet
    ├── kubectl quick reference
    ├── Terraform naming conventions
    └── Git workflow guide
```

### 6.5 Pair Programming & Mob Programming

- **Pair programming:** Two people work on a problem together — one drives (types), one navigates (thinks out loud)
- **Mob programming:** A whole group works on one problem together — great for complex issues
- **Why it works:** Knowledge transfers naturally through conversation

### 6.6 Show-and-Tell / Demo Days

- **Format:** 5-10 minute demos, multiple presenters
- **Rule:** Show, don't tell. Live demos > slides
- **Examples:**
  - "Look at this Grafana dashboard I built"
  - "Here's how I automated our backup process"
  - "Watch me deploy with ArgoCD in 30 seconds"

## 7. Internal DevOps Guilds & Working Groups

### What's a Guild?

A **guild** is a cross-team group of people who share a specific interest or expertise. Think of it as a **mini-community within the larger community**.

Spotify popularized this model:

```
                    Organization
                        │
        ┌───────────────┼───────────────┐
        │               │               │
    Squad A         Squad B         Squad C
   (Payments)      (Search)        (Mobile)
        │               │               │
        └───────┐       │       ┌───────┘
                │       │       │
              ┌─┴───────┴───────┴─┐
              │   DevOps Guild    │
              │   (Cross-team)    │
              └───────────────────┘
                Members from each
                squad who care
                about DevOps
```

### Types of Working Groups

| Working Group | Focus | Duration |
|---------------|-------|----------|
| **CI/CD Working Group** | Standardize pipeline templates across teams | 3 months |
| **Kubernetes Best Practices** | Define K8s standards, resource limits, security policies | Ongoing |
| **Observability Guild** | Improve monitoring, logging, and alerting | Ongoing |
| **Security Champions** | Bring DevSecOps practices to every team | Ongoing |
| **Migration Task Force** | Plan and execute cloud/tool migration | 6 months |
| **Developer Experience (DevEx)** | Improve tooling and workflows for developers | Ongoing |

### Running a Working Group

```yaml
# Working Group Charter

name: "CI/CD Standards Working Group"
goal: "Create standardized CI/CD pipeline templates that all teams can use"
duration: "3 months (Q3 2026)"

members:
  - lead: "Alice (DevOps Lead)"
  - "Bob (Backend Team)"
  - "Charlie (Frontend Team)"
  - "Dana (QA Team)"
  - "Eve (Security Team)"

deliverables:
  - "Shared Jenkins pipeline library"
  - "GitHub Actions reusable workflows"
  - "CI/CD best practices document"
  - "Pipeline security checklist"

meetings: "Weekly, 30 minutes, Tuesday 10 AM"

success_criteria:
  - "80% of teams adopt the shared templates"
  - "Average pipeline setup time drops from 2 days to 2 hours"
  - "Zero credential leaks in pipelines"
```

## 8. Organizing Internal Events

Events keep the community alive and energized. Here are event types you can organize:

### 8.1 Hackathons

- **Duration:** 1-2 days
- **Goal:** Build something useful — automate a pain point, try a new tool, improve existing tooling
- **Rules:** 
  - Teams of 3-5 people
  - Must demo at the end
  - Judging criteria: impact, creativity, feasibility
- **Prizes:** Don't need to be expensive — a trophy, bragging rights, or a team lunch

**Hackathon Theme Ideas:**
- "Automate the boring stuff" — Automate a manual process
- "Observability week" — Build dashboards and alerts for blind spots
- "Security sprint" — Fix security findings in your services
- "Developer experience" — Build internal tools that make devs' lives easier

### 8.2 Internal Conferences (Mini DevOpsDays)

Yes, you can run a mini-conference inside your company!

```
📅 Internal DevOpsDays — Agenda

09:00 — Opening Keynote: "Where We Are and Where We're Going"
         (VP of Engineering)

09:30 — Talk 1: "Our Kubernetes Journey — Lessons from Year 1"
         (Platform Team)

10:15 — Break ☕

10:30 — Talk 2: "How We Reduced Deployment Failures by 70%"
         (Backend Team)

11:15 — Lightning Talks (5 min each)
         • "Cool Bash One-Liners" — Alice
         • "Grafana Tips & Tricks" — Bob
         • "My Favorite kubectl Plugins" — Charlie

12:00 — Lunch 🍕

13:00 — Workshop 1: "Hands-On Terraform Modules" (Lab Room A)
         Workshop 2: "ArgoCD Deep Dive" (Lab Room B)

14:30 — Open Space / Unconference Sessions
         (Attendees propose and vote on topics)

15:30 — Panel Discussion: "DevOps in 2027 — What's Next?"

16:00 — Closing & Awards
```

### 8.3 Book Clubs

Pick a book. Read a chapter every week or two. Discuss.

**Recommended DevOps books:**
- 📕 *The Phoenix Project* — Gene Kim
- 📗 *The DevOps Handbook* — Gene Kim, Jez Humble, Patrick Debois
- 📘 *Accelerate* — Nicole Forsgren, Jez Humble, Gene Kim
- 📙 *The Unicorn Project* — Gene Kim
- 📒 *Site Reliability Engineering* — Google
- 📓 *Team Topologies* — Matthew Skelton, Manuel Pais
- 📔 *The Goal* — Eliyahu Goldratt

**Format:**
- Read 2-3 chapters before each meeting
- One person leads the discussion
- Discuss: "What resonated? What's applicable to us? What do we disagree with?"

### 8.4 Failure Fridays (Game Days)

- Deliberately break things in a staging/test environment
- Practice incident response
- Learn from the experience

```
🎮 Failure Friday — Game Day Plan

Scenario: "The primary database has stopped responding"

Teams:
  - Incident Commander: Alice
  - Communication Lead: Bob
  - Technical Responders: Charlie, Dana, Eve

Timeline:
  10:00 — Inject failure (secret from the facilitator)
  10:05 — Alert fires → Team assembles
  10:10 — Diagnosis begins
  10:30 — Root cause identified
  10:45 — Remediation applied
  11:00 — Post-mortem discussion
  11:30 — Wrap-up: What did we learn?
```

### 9.2 Mentorship Programs

Pair experienced DevOps practitioners with people who want to learn:

| Program Type | Description |
|-------------|-------------|

| **Rotation Programs** | Developer spends 2 weeks embedded with the DevOps team |

| **Reverse Mentoring** | Junior teaches senior about new tools/approaches |

### 9.3 Certification Study Groups

Study together for certifications:
- **CKA** — Certified Kubernetes Administrator
- **CKS** — Certified Kubernetes Security Specialist
- **AWS Solutions Architect**
- **Terraform Associate**
- **Docker Certified Associate**

**Format:**
- Weekly study sessions (1 hour)
- Practice questions together
- Share resources and notes
- Celebrate when people pass! 🎉

### 9.4 Innovation Time / 20% Time

Some companies give engineers time to work on projects outside their main work. Even if your company doesn't have an official policy, you can advocate for it:

- **Google's 20% time** — Spend 20% of time on side projects
- **Atlassian's ShipIt Days** — 24-hour hackathons, quarterly
- **3M's 15% time** — Led to Post-it Notes!

Propose to leadership:
> *"Give us 4 hours per month to work on DevOps improvements. We'll track the impact and report back."*

## 11. Measuring Community Success

### Quantitative Metrics

|--------|-------------------|
| **Active members** | How many people regularly participate |
| **Event attendance** | Are people showing up? |
| **Slack messages / posts** | Is the community active? |
| **Knowledge articles created** | Is knowledge being documented? |
| **Cross-team collaborations** | Are silos breaking down? |
| **Incident MTTR** | Is knowledge sharing reducing recovery time? |
| **Deployment frequency** | Are best practices being adopted? |
| **Onboarding time** | Are new hires ramping up faster? |

- **Survey responses:** "How valuable do you find the DevOps community?" (1-10)

- **Feedback:** Regularly ask "What's working? What's not?"

## 12. Handling Resistance & Skepticism

Not everyone will be excited about your community. That's normal. Here's how to handle common pushbacks:

### "I Don't Have Time for This"

**Response:** *"I totally understand. The community is designed to SAVE you time in the long run. Instead of spending 3 hours debugging a problem alone, you can ask in Slack and get an answer in 10 minutes. Start by just lurking in the channel — you'll be surprised how much you pick up."*

### "We Already Have a DevOps Team. Why Do We Need a Community?"

**Response:** *"The DevOps team builds and maintains the platform. The community helps EVERYONE understand and use it better. It's like having a gym (the platform) vs. having a fitness community (people who motivate each other to stay healthy)."*

### "This Sounds Like More Meetings"

**Response:** *"We keep events optional and focused. One 45-minute session every two weeks. And we record everything, so if you can't attend live, you can watch later. We also do a lot asynchronously on Slack."*

### "Management Won't Support This"

**Response:** *"Let's start small and prove the value first. Run a few informal sessions, track the impact, then present the results to management. Hard to argue with 'we reduced incident recovery time by 40%.'"*

### "Nobody's Going to Present / Nobody Will Come"

**Response:** *"Start with the willing. Even 5 people in a room sharing ideas is a community. People will join when they see the value. And for presentations — start with conversations, not formal talks. 'Hey, can you show us how you did that thing with Terraform?' is a presentation."*

### The Key Principle:

> **Don't argue. Demonstrate value. Let the results speak.**

## 13. Scaling the Community

Once your community has momentum, here's how to scale it:

### Phase 1: Seed (0-10 members)
- Informal conversations
- One Slack channel
- Coffee chats

### Phase 2: Sprout (10-30 members)
- Regular bi-weekly meetups
- Start recording talks
- First internal blog posts
- Assign 2-3 champions

### Phase 3: Grow (30-100 members)
- Multiple channels for different topics
- Working groups for specific initiatives
- Internal knowledge base / wiki
- Mentorship program starts
- Quarterly hackathons

### Phase 4: Thrive (100+ members)
- Multiple guilds (K8s guild, CI/CD guild, Security guild)
- Internal conference (mini DevOpsDays)
- Formal learning paths
- Dedicated community lead (part-time or full-time)
- Cross-location / cross-region communities
- Community newsletter

### Scaling Tips

1. **Decentralize leadership** — Don't be a bottleneck. Empower champions.
2. **Create templates** — Make it easy for anyone to host a session.
3. **Automate the boring stuff** — Use bots for reminders, scheduling, and summaries.
4. **Regional chapters** — If your company is global, have local community leads.
5. **Self-service resources** — People should be able to learn without waiting for an event.

## 14. Real-World Examples & Case Studies

### Case Study 1: Spotify — The Guild Model

**What they did:**
- Organized engineering into **Squads** (small, autonomous teams)
- Created **Guilds** — cross-team groups for shared interests
- DevOps, Testing, and Data Engineering each had their own guild
- Guilds met regularly to share knowledge and standardize practices

**Result:**
- Faster knowledge sharing across 250+ engineers
- Reduced duplication of effort
- Created a strong engineering culture

### Case Study 2: Google — SRE Community

**What they did:**
- Built a massive internal SRE community
- Published the "SRE Book" publicly (sharing externally too!)
- Internal training programs, mentorship, and rotation programs
- Regular postmortems shared across the entire company

**Result:**
- World-class reliability (99.99%+ uptime)
- SRE became a career path, not just a role
- Culture of learning from failures without blame

### Case Study 3: ING Bank — DevOps Transformation

**What they did:**
- Reorganized 3,500 engineers into cross-functional squads
- Created DevOps guilds for knowledge sharing
- Invested heavily in internal training
- Ran regular hackathons and innovation days

**Result:**
- Release frequency increased 10x
- Time to market reduced from months to weeks
- Employee satisfaction significantly improved

### Case Study 4: Target — Inner Source Community

**What they did:**
- Applied open-source principles internally ("inner source")
- Engineers could contribute to any team's codebase
- Created an internal GitHub-like platform for collaboration
- DevOps tools and templates were shared company-wide

**Result:**
- Reduced tool fragmentation
- Teams built on each other's work instead of reinventing
- Faster innovation cycles

## 15. Common Pitfalls & How to Avoid Them

| Pitfall | What Happens | How to Avoid |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| **Starting too big** | You try to launch with a big event, nobody comes, you're discouraged | Start with 3-5 people. Grow organically. |
| **One person does everything** | The community lead burns out and the community dies | Distribute responsibility. Build a team of champions. |
| **Only senior people speak** | Junior engineers feel intimidated and don't participate | Actively invite juniors to present. Create a safe space. |
| **Too formal / corporate** | People feel like it's another mandatory meeting | Keep it casual. Make it fun. Bring snacks. |
| **No follow-through** | Great ideas in meetings, but nothing happens after | Assign action items with owners and deadlines. Track them. |
| **Ignoring remote/async folks** | Only in-office people participate | Record everything. Maintain async channels. Rotate meeting times. |
| **Not celebrating wins** | People don't feel appreciated and stop contributing | Shout out contributors regularly. Small wins matter. |
| **Making it mandatory** | Forcing people kills intrinsic motivation | Keep it voluntary. Make people WANT to come. |
| **Talking without doing** | All discussion, no action | Every meeting should have at least one actionable outcome |
| **Not getting leadership support** | You're fighting uphill without resources or time allocation | Show results early. Frame it in business terms. |

## 17. Summary

### Key Takeaways

| # | Takeaway |
|---|----------|
| 1 | **DevOps is a culture, not a tool** — Communities make that culture real |
| 2 | **Start small** — 3-5 passionate people is all you need |
| 3 | **Champions are everything** — Find them, empower them, recognize them |
| 4 | **Make knowledge sharing easy** — Blogs, wikis, talks, Slack channels |
| 5 | **Keep it fun and voluntary** — Forced communities die |
| 6 | **Measure impact** — Track metrics, but value stories too |
| 7 | **Handle resistance with results** — Don't argue, demonstrate value |
| 8 | **Scale deliberately** — Grow the community in phases |
| 9 | **Invest in learning** — Mentorship, certifications, learning paths |
| 10 | **Be patient** — Culture change takes time. Stay consistent. |

### The Community Maturity Model

```
Level 1: Informal       → "A few people chat on Slack about DevOps"
Level 2: Organized      → "Regular meetups with a small core group"
Level 3: Established    → "Formal CoP with champions, events, and knowledge base"
Level 4: Thriving       → "Multiple guilds, hackathons, mentorship, internal conferences"
Level 5: Transformative → "DevOps culture is embedded in the organization's DNA"
```

> **Remember:** You don't need to be a manager, a director, or a VP to start a community. You just need to care enough to begin. **The best communities start with someone who says, "Hey, let's talk about this over coffee."**
